The Perfect 7-Day Kyushu Road Trip Itinerary

Seven days. Seven prefectures. One unforgettable loop around Japan's volcanic southern island — with every onsen, every scenic drive, and every overnight stop planned for you.

✍️
VANTRIPJAPAN Team
14 min read

Seven days is the sweet spot for a Kyushu campervan road trip. It is long enough to experience the island's extraordinary diversity — active volcanoes, black-sand beaches, thousand-year-old hot springs, sacred gorges, subtropical coastlines — without the rushed, checkbox-ticking feeling of trying to see everything in a weekend. This itinerary traces a counter-clockwise loop starting and ending in Fukuoka, covering approximately 1,100 kilometers of the most rewarding roads on the island.

Every overnight stop has been chosen for practicality: free or low-cost parking, proximity to onsen, and ease of access for campervans. Every day includes a mix of driving, sightseeing, and soaking — the three pillars of a great Kyushu road trip. The daily driving distances are deliberately kept moderate (averaging 150–200 km per day) to allow time for spontaneous detours, leisurely meals, and the long, meditative baths that are the whole point of traveling in this part of Japan.

This itinerary assumes you pick up your campervan in Fukuoka in the morning and return it seven days later. Let's begin.

Day 1: Fukuoka → Beppu

Distance: ~160 km  |  Driving time: 2–2.5 hours (via expressway) or 3–4 hours (via coastal route)  |  Overnight: Beppu area

Pick up your campervan from VanTripJapan near Fukuoka Airport in the morning. Before leaving Fukuoka, stock up on supplies at a supermarket — we recommend the large AEON or Costco near the airport for a comprehensive shop. Fill your cooler with drinks, pick up rice, basic condiments, and snacks for the road.

Head east toward Beppu. If time permits, take the scenic coastal route via National Route 10 through Kitakyushu and along the northern Oita coast, rather than the expressway. This route passes through Nakatsu (famous for its karaage fried chicken — pull over at any roadside shop for some of the best you will ever eat) and along the Kunisaki Peninsula coast.

Arrive in Beppu in the early afternoon and head directly to the Kannawa district. Spend the afternoon exploring the Jigoku Meguri (Hell Tour) — a circuit of spectacularly colored geothermal pools. The cobalt-blue Umi Jigoku and the blood-red Chinoike Jigoku are the most photogenic. Allow 2–3 hours for the full circuit.

In the evening, visit Hyōtan Onsen for a comprehensive bathing experience — indoor baths, outdoor baths, sand baths, and steam baths, all in one facility (approximately ¥800). The complex stays open until 1:00 AM, so there is no rush.

Dinner suggestion: Try jigoku-mushi (hell-steamed) cooking at Kannawa's Jigoku Mushi Kobo, where you steam your own seafood and vegetables in natural volcanic steam. It is a uniquely Beppu experience.

Overnight parking: Beppu Park parking area or the Kannawa public parking lot. Both are spacious and accommodate campervans comfortably.

Day 2: Beppu → Yufuin → Kurokawa Onsen

Distance: ~100 km  |  Driving time: 2–2.5 hours  |  Overnight: Kurokawa Onsen area

Start your morning with a drive to Yufuin (30 minutes from Beppu). Park at the large public lot near the station and walk the charming main street, Yunotsubo Kaido, toward Kinrin Lake. If you depart early enough, you will catch the ethereal morning mist rising from the thermal lake — a scene that epitomizes Yufuin's delicate beauty.

Browse the galleries, artisan shops, and cafés along the street. Pick up a fresh-baked roll from B-speak (Yufuin's famous roll cake shop — arrive before they sell out, usually by early afternoon) and an espresso from one of the town's independent coffee roasters.

After lunch, begin the drive south on the Yamanami Highway — arguably the most beautiful road in Kyushu. This mountain road crosses the Kuju highlands, passing through vast alpine grasslands and offering panoramic views from Makinoto Pass (1,330m). Stop at every viewpoint. This drive is the reason you rented a campervan.

Rather than continuing all the way to Aso, turn off toward Kurokawa Onsen, a tiny hot spring village hidden in a mountain gorge. Purchase a Nyūtō Tegata wooden pass (¥1,300) and visit three different ryokan baths. Recommended: Yamabiko Ryokan's cave bath and Shinmei-kan's carved-tunnel bath. The village is magical at dusk, when lanterns light the narrow paths along the river.

Dinner suggestion: Many of Kurokawa's ryokan restaurants serve dinner to non-guests. Local river fish (yamame trout) grilled over charcoal is a specialty.

Overnight parking: Kurokawa public parking lot at the village entrance, or Michi-no-Eki Oguni (approximately 20 minutes away) for a quieter night.

Day 3: Kurokawa → Aso → Takachiho

Distance: ~120 km  |  Driving time: 2.5–3 hours  |  Overnight: Takachiho area

Drive south from Kurokawa into the Aso caldera. The first stop is Daikanbo (大観峰), a viewpoint on the northern caldera rim that provides a breathtaking panorama of the entire caldera below — farmland, villages, and the central volcanic cone of Mount Aso rising from the middle like a primordial fortress. This is widely considered the single best viewpoint in Kyushu.

Descend into the caldera and drive the Aso Panorama Line south toward the volcano. Stop at Kusasenri (草千里ヶ浜), the vast circular meadow with wild horses and a reflecting pond, set against the backdrop of the smoking volcano. If conditions permit (check volcanic alert levels), continue to the Mount Nakadake crater observation point to peer into the turquoise crater lake.

After lunch at one of the restaurants in Aso town (try the local aka-ushi red beef — a regional specialty served as steak, rice bowls, or burgers), head east toward Takachiho.

Arrive in Takachiho in the late afternoon and walk down to the gorge. Rent a rowboat (¥5,100 for 30 minutes, up to 3 people) and paddle beneath the Manai Falls as they cascade 17 meters into the narrow basalt canyon. The hexagonal rock columns and emerald water create a scene of otherworldly beauty.

In the evening, attend the Takachiho Yokagura (高千穂夜神楽) at the Takachiho Shrine — a nightly performance of sacred Shinto dances depicting the myths of the gods, performed by firelight. Shows run from 20:00 to 21:00 and cost ¥1,000.

Overnight parking: Takachiho public parking area near the gorge entrance. The town is quiet at night and the parking is spacious.

Day 4: Takachiho → Miyazaki → Nichinan Coast

Distance: ~200 km  |  Driving time: 3.5–4 hours  |  Overnight: Nichinan coast area

This is the longest driving day, but the scenery rewards every kilometer. Begin by visiting the Amano Iwato Shrine (天岩戸神社) on the outskirts of Takachiho — the shrine marking the cave where the sun goddess Amaterasu allegedly hid. Ask the shrine priests for a guided walk to the Amano Yasukawara cave across the river, where thousands of hand-stacked stone cairns create an almost supernatural atmosphere.

Head south through the mountains toward the Pacific coast. The road descends through increasingly lush, subtropical forest before reaching Miyazaki City. Stop briefly for lunch — Miyazaki's signature dish is chicken nanban (fried chicken with vinegar-sweet sauce and tartar sauce). Ogura Honten, the restaurant that invented the dish, is a local institution.

After lunch, begin driving the Nichinan Coast south along National Route 220. Your first stop is Aoshima Island — cross the bridge to this tiny tropical island surrounded by the "Devil's Washboard" rock formations and visit the colorful Aoshima Shrine. Continue south to Sun Messe Nichinan for the surreal sight of seven Moai statues overlooking the Pacific.

If time permits, detour to the historic samurai town of Obi (飫肥) for a walk through its atmospheric streets and castle grounds. Try the local obi-ten fish cakes from the street vendors.

Dinner suggestion: Fresh sashimi at any of the fishing port restaurants along the coast. Miyazaki's Pacific coast is famous for katsuo (bonito) and maguro (tuna).

Overnight parking: Michi-no-Eki Nango (道の駅 なんごう) on the coast offers beautiful overnight parking with subtropical gardens and ocean views.

Day 5: Nichinan Coast → Kagoshima → Ibusuki

Distance: ~180 km  |  Driving time: 3–3.5 hours  |  Overnight: Ibusuki area

Drive south and west toward Kagoshima Prefecture. The landscape shifts from the lush Pacific coast to the volcanic terrain of southern Kyushu. As you approach Kagoshima City, Sakurajima — one of the world's most active volcanoes — comes into view across Kinko Bay, its cone perpetually wreathed in a thin haze of volcanic emission.

Take the Sakurajima Ferry from Kagoshima Port (15 minutes, runs every 15 minutes, campervans welcome at approximately ¥1,600). Drive the loop road around the volcano, stopping at the Yunohira Observatory for the closest public view of the smoking crater, and the Kurokami Buried Shrine Gate — a torii buried up to its crossbar by the massive 1914 eruption. Return by the same ferry.

Continue south along the western shore of Kinko Bay to Ibusuki (指宿). The highlight here is the famous natural sand bath at the Sunamushi Kaikan (砂むし会館 砂楽). Change into a yukata, lie on the volcanic beach, and be buried in naturally heated sand (50–55°C) for 10–15 minutes. The sensation is extraordinary — a deep, penetrating warmth followed by a profound relaxation. Admission is approximately ¥1,200.

After the sand bath, rinse off in the facility's conventional onsen, then head to Cape Nagasakibana at the tip of the Satsuma Peninsula for sunset views of Kaimon-dake ("Satsuma Fuji") rising from the sea.

Dinner suggestion: Kagoshima is famous for kurobuta (Berkshire pork). Look for a tonkatsu restaurant serving kurobuta — the meat is noticeably sweeter and more tender than standard pork.

Overnight parking: The waterfront area near the Sunamushi Kaikan has public parking. Alternatively, Michi-no-Eki Ibusuki or nearby Michi-no-Eki along the coast.

Day 6: Ibusuki → Kirishima → Kumamoto

Distance: ~210 km  |  Driving time: 3.5–4 hours  |  Overnight: Kumamoto area

Head north from Ibusuki into the mountains toward the Kirishima volcanic range. The drive takes you through some of Kagoshima's most fertile agricultural land — endless fields of sweet potatoes, tea plantations, and the occasional shochu distillery.

Arrive at Kirishima Onsen by late morning. Visit the Kirishima Jingu Shrine (霧島神宮), one of southern Kyushu's most important and atmospheric Shinto shrines, set among towering ancient cedar trees with vermillion-lacquered buildings. The spiritual weight of this place is palpable.

For bathing, choose between the grand Kirishima Hotel — home to one of Japan's largest hot spring pools — or the rustic Shinyu Onsen (新燃荘), where milky-blue sulfurous water fills outdoor baths in a mountain forest. Both offer day-use bathing for approximately ¥1,000.

After lunch, drive northwest to Kumamoto. Arrive in the late afternoon and visit Kumamoto Castle (熊本城), one of Japan's three premier castles. The castle was significantly damaged in the 2016 earthquakes, and the ongoing restoration is a remarkable engineering feat — you can view both the restored sections and the still-damaged stone walls from elevated walkways. The juxtaposition of destruction and restoration is deeply moving.

Spend the evening exploring Kumamoto's Shimotori shopping arcade and dining district. This is one of Kyushu's most vibrant nightlife areas.

Dinner suggestion: Kumamoto's signature dish is basashi (raw horse meat sashimi), served thinly sliced with grated ginger and soy sauce. If that is too adventurous, try Kumamoto ramen — thick noodles in a rich, garlicky tonkotsu broth with ma-yu (blackened garlic oil).

Overnight parking: Multiple public parking areas near Kumamoto Castle. Michi-no-Eki in the Kumamoto suburbs are also excellent options.

Day 7: Kumamoto → Nagasaki → Fukuoka

Distance: ~250 km  |  Driving time: 4–4.5 hours (total)  |  Return: Fukuoka

Your final day covers the most ground, but the route is primarily expressway and the driving is straightforward. Depart Kumamoto in the morning and head west toward Nagasaki (approximately 2 hours via the expressway).

Nagasaki is a city of profound historical significance. Spend the morning at the Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum and the adjacent Peace Park. The museum's exhibits are harrowing, meticulously documented, and deeply important. Allow at least 90 minutes for a proper visit.

After the museum, walk through the Glover Garden (グラバー園), a hillside park of Western-style mansions built by European traders in the 19th century, offering panoramic views over Nagasaki's harbor. The city's unique history as Japan's sole point of contact with the West during its 200-year period of isolation has left it with a distinctive architectural and cultural character unlike anywhere else in Japan.

For lunch, Nagasaki's culinary specialties are unmissable: champon (a rich noodle soup with pork, seafood, and vegetables, influenced by Chinese and Portuguese cooking) and castella (a Portuguese-style sponge cake that Nagasaki has been producing since the 16th century). Shikairō restaurant in Chinatown is the birthplace of champon.

After lunch, begin the return drive to Fukuoka (approximately 2 hours via the Nagasaki Expressway). If you have time, consider a brief stop in Dazaifu on the outskirts of Fukuoka to visit Dazaifu Tenmangu Shrine, one of Japan's most important shrines dedicated to the god of learning. The approach street is lined with mochi shops selling umegae mochi (plum-shaped rice cakes grilled with sweet red bean filling).

Return your campervan to VanTripJapan near Fukuoka Airport. If your flight is the following day, consider spending your final night exploring Fukuoka's legendary yatai (outdoor food stalls) along the Naka River in Tenjin or at Nakasu Island. These tiny stalls serve ramen, yakitori, and oden in a convivial atmosphere that perfectly caps a week of extraordinary travel.

Itinerary Summary

  • Day 1: Fukuoka → Beppu (160 km) — Jigoku Meguri, Hyōtan Onsen
  • Day 2: Beppu → Yufuin → Kurokawa (100 km) — Yamanami Highway, cave baths
  • Day 3: Kurokawa → Aso → Takachiho (120 km) — Daikanbo, Kusasenri, gorge boating, Yokagura
  • Day 4: Takachiho → Miyazaki → Nichinan (200 km) — Amano Iwato, Aoshima, Moai, Obi
  • Day 5: Nichinan → Kagoshima → Ibusuki (180 km) — Sakurajima loop, sand baths
  • Day 6: Ibusuki → Kirishima → Kumamoto (210 km) — Kirishima Shrine, onsen, castle
  • Day 7: Kumamoto → Nagasaki → Fukuoka (250 km) — Peace Museum, Glover Garden, champon

Total distance: Approximately 1,220 km

Budget Estimate (Per Person, Per Day)

  • Fuel: ¥2,000–¥3,000 (campervans average 8–10 km/L)
  • Onsen / bathing: ¥500–¥1,500
  • Food: ¥2,000–¥4,000 (mix of supermarket meals and restaurants)
  • Tolls: ¥0–¥2,000 (depending on expressway usage; free routes available throughout)
  • Attractions: ¥500–¥1,500
  • Total daily estimate: ¥5,000–¥12,000 per person (excluding campervan rental)

Packing Essentials

  • A small towel for onsen. Many onsen provide soap and shampoo, but you will want your own quick-dry towel.
  • Cash. Rural Kyushu is still largely cash-based. Many Michi-no-Eki, small restaurants, and public onsen do not accept credit cards. Withdraw yen at a 7-Eleven or Japan Post ATM before leaving Fukuoka.
  • A cooler bag. Essential for keeping drinks and perishables fresh. Stock up at supermarkets along the route.
  • Layers. Temperatures can vary by 15°C between the coast and the mountain passes, even in summer.
  • A Japanese translation app. English signage improves yearly but is still limited in rural areas. Google Translate's camera function is invaluable for menus and signs.

Seven days. One island. A thousand memories waiting in volcanic steam, coastal sunsets, and winding mountain roads. This is not just a road trip — it is an immersion into one of the most geologically dramatic, culturally rich, and culinarily extraordinary regions on Earth. Your campervan is the key. Kyushu is the destination. All that remains is to turn the ignition and begin.

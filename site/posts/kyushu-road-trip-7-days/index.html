The Perfect 7-Day Kyushu Road Trip Itinerary

Seven days. Seven prefectures. One unforgettable loop around Japan's volcanic southern island — with every onsen, every scenic drive, and every overnight stop planned for you.

✍️
Karen
15 min read

Hi there, I’m Karen! Along with my husband, we run VanTripJapan here in Fukuoka. Over the years, we’ve welcomed hundreds of international guests to our home island, and I’m always asked: "How long do we need to really experience Kyushu?" My honest answer is that **seven days is the absolute sweet spot** for a campervan road trip. It gives you enough time to explore our volcanic peaks, hidden hot springs, ocean drives, and sacred valleys without feeling like you are constantly racing against the clock.

When we handbuild and maintain our custom campervans, we keep rural Japan's narrow mountain passes in mind. This loop represents a counter-clockwise circle of approximately 1,100 kilometers. Every day combines beautiful driving, delicious local food, and relaxing baths (onsen). Here is my personal, local guide to the ultimate week in Kyushu.

Day 1: Fukuoka → Beppu

Distance: ~160 km  |  Driving time: 2.5 hours  |  Overnight: Beppu area

When you pick up your campervan from our Fukuoka Airport base (just 10 minutes from the terminal!), we will spend some time showing you all the features of your new home-on-wheels. Once you are comfortable, I recommend driving east toward Beppu, Japan’s hot spring capital. If you want a scenic coastal drive, skip the expressway and take National Route 10 through Nakatsu (famous for its incredible karaage fried chicken!).

Arrive in Beppu in the afternoon and head straight to the geothermal district of Kannawa. Walk between the steaming "Hells" (Jigoku Meguri)—the cobalt-blue Umi Jigoku is my absolute favorite. Before heading to sleep, make sure to soak in the sand and steam baths at Hyōtan Onsen, which is open until 1:00 AM.

Karen's Local Tip: Hell-Steam Cooking (Jigoku-mushi)

For dinner, visit the Jigoku Mushi Kobo in Kannawa. Here, you use the town's natural volcanic steam vents to cook fresh local seafood, pork, and vegetables in wooden steaming baskets. It is super healthy, delicious, and incredibly fun!

Overnight parking: Beppu Park parking area or the public Kannawa lot. Both are quiet, flat, and spacious enough for campervans.

Day 2: Beppu → Yufuin → Kurokawa Onsen

Distance: ~100 km  |  Driving time: 2.5 hours  |  Overnight: Kurokawa Onsen area

Depart early and climb the mountain road to Yufuin (35 minutes away). If you get there early, you’ll catch Kinrin Lake draped in its famous morning mist. Take a walk along Yunotsubo Kaido street, pick up a roll cake from B-speak, and enjoy Yufuin's artsy vibe.

In the afternoon, you will drive the legendary Yamanami Highway across the Kuju highlands. My husband and I specifically chose and designed our campervans to be narrow and compact so you can climb scenic mountain passes like Makinoto Pass (1,330m) without any stress. The views of the gold and green highlands are spectacular!

End your drive in the hidden ryokan village of Kurokawa Onsen, tucked away in a forested river gorge. It is one of the most traditional hot spring towns in all of Japan.

Kurokawa Onsen Bath Pass (Nyūtō Tegata)

Purchase a wooden pass (Nyūtō Tegata) at the local information center for ¥1,300. This lets you choose and enter three different outdoor baths (rotenburo) at various ryokans. I highly recommend Yamabiko Ryokan's giant riverside pool and Shinmei-kan's carved cave bath.

Overnight parking: Kurokawa village public lot, or drive 20 minutes to Michi-no-Eki Oguni for a quiet, scenic rest stop.

Day 3: Kurokawa → Aso → Takachiho

Distance: ~120 km  |  Driving time: 3 hours  |  Overnight: Takachiho area

Today we head down into the colossal **Aso Caldera**—the largest active volcanic caldera in the world. Stop first at Daikanbo (大観峰) on the northern rim. Standing on this grassy cliff looking down at the villages and active peaks in the center of the crater is a spiritual experience. I never tire of this view!

Drive across the caldera floor, pass the Kusasenri grasslands (where wild horses roam), and visit the active Nakadake crater (always check the safety alert status first). Grab lunch in Aso town—the local **Aka-ushi** (red beef) rice bowls are world-famous.

In the afternoon, drive east into Miyazaki Prefecture to the mythical **Takachiho Gorge**. Rent a rowboat and paddle down the emerald waters of the basalt canyon directly under the cascading Manai Falls. At 8:00 PM, head to Takachiho Shrine to watch the nightly Shinto theatrical dance performance (Yokagura)—it is a fascinating window into ancient Japanese legends.

Overnight parking: Takachiho Gorge public parking area (quiet and very safe) or the nearby campsite.

Day 4: Takachiho → Miyazaki → Nichinan Coast

Distance: ~200 km  |  Driving time: 4 hours  |  Overnight: Nichinan Coast area

Begin your morning at the sacred **Amano Iwato Shrine**, where local priests can show you the cave where the sun goddess Amaterasu reportedly hid. The path along the river leading to the stone-stacking cave of Amano Yasukawara is incredibly serene.

From there, drive south through the mountains to the tropical Pacific coast. Make sure to stop in Miyazaki City for lunch to taste **Chicken Nanban** (fried chicken dipped in vinegar sauce and topped with tartar sauce). I highly recommend Ogura Honten, the birthplace of the dish!

Continue south along the palm-lined **Nichinan Coast**, stopping at Aoshima Island (surrounded by the unique rock ridges called "Devil's Washboard") and Sun Messe Nichinan (which has official replicas of the Moai statues overlooking the blue ocean).

Karen's Favorite Overnight Spot: Michi-no-Eki Nango

Drive slightly further south to Michi-no-Eki Nango. It sits on a cliff surrounded by subtropical gardens. Waking up to the sunrise over the Pacific Ocean here is one of the most memorable mornings of any Kyushu trip. Overnight stays are highly tolerated if you follow basic manners (no camping gear outside the van!).

Day 5: Nichinan Coast → Kagoshima → Ibusuki

Distance: ~180 km  |  Driving time: 3.5 hours  |  Overnight: Ibusuki area

Head west toward Kagoshima. The views change as you approach Kinko Bay and see the massive, active peak of **Sakurajima** venting ash. Take the Sakurajima Ferry across the bay—it takes only 15 minutes and is a breeze to board with our campervans (cost is around ¥1,600).

Drive around the base of the volcano, visit the Kurokami buried shrine gate (buried in ash during the massive 1914 eruption), and head south down the Satsuma Peninsula to the seaside resort of **Ibusuki**.

Ibusuki is famous for its **natural sand baths** (Saraku Sand Bath Hall). You wear a yukata, lie down on the beach, and the staff buries you in volcanically heated sand for 15 minutes. It is a strange but incredibly detoxifying sensation that will make you sleep like a baby!

Overnight parking: Waterfront public parking near the sand bath hall, or the nearby Michi-no-Eki Ibusuki.

Day 6: Ibusuki → Kirishima → Kumamoto

Distance: ~210 km  |  Driving time: 4 hours  |  Overnight: Kumamoto area

Drive north through Kagoshima’s scenic green tea plantations into the **Kirishima volcanic range**. Stop at **Kirishima Jingu Shrine**, a vibrant red complex hidden among giant, thousand-year-old cedar trees. For bathing, you can stop at Kirishima Hotel’s massive hot spring pool or enjoy the milky-blue sulfur waters of Shinyu Onsen.

In the afternoon, take the expressway northwest to **Kumamoto**. Visit **Kumamoto Castle**, one of the most imposing fortresses in Japan. Seeing the restoration progress since the devastating 2016 earthquakes is incredibly moving. In the evening, explore the Shimotori shopping arcade—it's the best spot to try Kumamoto’s famous **garlicky tonkotsu ramen** or basashi (horse meat sashimi).

Overnight parking: Public lots near Kumamoto Castle, or Michi-no-Eki in the Kumamoto suburbs.

Day 7: Kumamoto → Nagasaki → Fukuoka

Distance: ~250 km  |  Driving time: 4.5 hours  |  Return: Fukuoka Base

On your final day, head west to the historic port city of **Nagasaki** (about 2 hours via the expressway). Nagasaki has a unique European-influenced culture from its 200-year history as Japan's only open trade port during isolation. Visit the hillside Glover Garden to see 19th-century colonial mansions, and spend time at the Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum and Peace Park.

Grab a bowl of **Nagasaki Champon** (a rich noodle soup packed with seafood and vegetables) for lunch, then take the Nagasaki Expressway back to Fukuoka (about 2 hours) to return your campervan to our Hakozaki Base.

Estimated Daily Budget (Per Person)

  • Fuel: ¥2,000–¥3,000 (our vans get 8–10 km/L)
  • Onsen: ¥500–¥1,500
  • Food: ¥2,000–¥4,000 (straight from local supermarkets and direct farmers' markets)
  • Tolls: ¥0–¥2,000 (we include a free ETC card reader, and you can easily choose toll-free routes on your GPS)
  • Total: ¥5,000–¥12,000 (excluding campervan rental)

Karen's Packing Essentials for Vanlife

  • Onsen Towel: Always carry a small quick-dry towel in the van. Many public hot springs do not provide them for free.
  • Cash (Yen): Many rural onsens, fruit stands, and roadside stations do not accept credit cards. Withdraw cash before leaving Fukuoka.
  • Layers: Mountain passes like Aso can be 15°C cooler than coastal areas, even in summer. Pack a jacket!

Seven days. One loop. And a lifetime of memories. Traveling Kyushu by campervan is not just a holiday—it’s an immersion into Japan’s most active, warm, and authentic region. We can't wait to hand you the keys to your adventure. Turn the ignition, and welcome to our home!

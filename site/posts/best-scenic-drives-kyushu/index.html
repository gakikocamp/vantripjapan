7 Breathtaking Scenic Drives in Kyushu

Mountain passes that disappear into clouds. Coastal roads carved into volcanic cliffs. Emerald valleys untouched by mass tourism. These are the driving routes that make Kyushu Japan's ultimate road trip destination.

✍️
VANTRIPJAPAN Team
11 min read

Japan's southernmost main island is a masterpiece of geological drama. Kyushu was forged by volcanic forces that continue to shape its landscape today — forces that have created some of the most visually spectacular driving roads in all of Asia. Unlike the famous Alpine routes of Europe or the coastal highways of California, Kyushu's scenic drives remain remarkably uncrowded, even during peak season. You can drive for an hour along a ridgeline above the clouds and encounter only a handful of other vehicles.

For campervan travelers, Kyushu offers something even more valuable than beautiful roads: the freedom to stop wherever the view demands it. There is no tour bus schedule. No train timetable. When you round a corner and find yourself staring at an active volcano framed by rolling grasslands, you simply pull over, open the side door of your van, and make coffee. That is the essence of a Kyushu road trip.

The seven routes below represent the finest scenic drives on the island, organized roughly from north to south. Each listing includes the route distance, estimated driving time (without stops), key highlights, and practical campervan considerations.

1. Yamanami Highway (やまなみハイウェイ)

Route: Beppu / Yufuin → Aso  |  Distance: ~65 km  |  Driving time: 1.5–2 hours

The Yamanami Highway is, by near-universal consensus, the single most beautiful driving road in Kyushu — and a strong contender for the finest in all of Japan. Officially designated as Oita Prefectural Route 11 and Kumamoto Prefectural Route 11, this mountain highway crosses the volcanic highlands of the Kuju mountain range, connecting the hot spring towns of Yufuin and Beppu in the east to the Aso caldera in the west.

The drive begins with a gentle ascent through Yufuin's pastoral basin, passing rice paddies and dairy farms with Mount Yufu towering behind you. As you climb, the landscape transforms dramatically. Dense forest gives way to open alpine grassland — vast rolling meadows of native susuki (pampas grass) and wildflowers that stretch to the horizon in every direction. In spring, these meadows blaze with the pink of miyama kirishima (mountain azaleas). In autumn, the susuki turns golden, swaying in waves under clear blue skies.

The highest point of the drive, Makinoto Pass (牧ノ戸峠, elevation 1,330m), offers a panoramic view that is genuinely staggering — the entire Kuju mountain range spreads before you, with multiple volcanic peaks rising above the grasslands. This is also the trailhead for the Kuju Mountain hiking routes, including the path to Mount Kuju (1,787m), the highest peak in Kyushu.

As you descend toward Aso, the road passes through Tadewara Marsh (タデ原湿原), a pristine wetland that is a registered Ramsar site, before opening up to reveal the immense Aso caldera — a natural amphitheater 25 kilometers wide that contains farms, villages, and an active volcanic cone at its center.

Campervan tips: The road is two lanes throughout and well-maintained, with gentle curves suitable for all campervan sizes. Multiple pull-off areas and parking lots are located at scenic viewpoints. The best light is in the early morning or late afternoon. Fog can reduce visibility on the passes in early morning; wait for it to lift for the best experience.

2. Aso Panorama Line & Kusasenri (阿蘇パノラマライン)

Route: Aso Town → Mount Aso Crater  |  Distance: ~20 km  |  Driving time: 40–60 minutes

If the Yamanami Highway is Kyushu's most beautiful road, the Aso Panorama Line is its most dramatic. This road climbs from the floor of the Aso caldera directly up the flanks of Mount Nakadake, one of the world's most accessible active volcanoes, to a crater rim observation point where you can peer into the churning, sulfurous crater below.

The drive begins on the caldera floor, passing through the town of Aso with its pleasant shops and restaurants. As you begin the ascent, the vegetation changes rapidly — cultivated fields give way to wild grassland, then to bare volcanic rock. The star attraction en route is Kusasenri (草千里ヶ浜), a vast circular meadow formed in an ancient crater. In the center sits a small, perfectly round pond reflecting the sky, surrounded by grazing horses. It is a landscape that looks more like Mongolia than Japan.

Beyond Kusasenri, the road climbs to the Aso Volcano Museum area and, conditions permitting, continues to the crater rim of Nakadake. When the volcano is in a quiet phase, you can stand at the edge and look down into the vivid turquoise crater lake, with sulfurous steam hissing from vents. Note that access to the crater is regularly restricted based on volcanic activity levels — check the Aso Volcano Disaster Prevention Council website before driving up.

Campervan tips: The road to Kusasenri is manageable for all campervans. The road from Kusasenri to the crater has some steep sections but is paved and has a large parking area at the top. During spring and autumn weekends, the Kusasenri parking area can fill up; arrive before 10:00 AM for the best spots. The panorama line has multiple routes (north, south, east); the southern route via the Aso Volcano Museum is the most scenic.

3. Takachiho Gorge Drive (高千穂峡ドライブ)

Route: Aso → Takachiho → Nobeoka Coast  |  Distance: ~90 km  |  Driving time: 2–2.5 hours

This route connects the volcanic highlands of Aso to one of Japan's most sacred and visually spectacular natural sites: Takachiho Gorge (高千穂峡). The drive itself is a winding journey through Kyushu's mountainous interior, passing through forests of cedar and cypress, crossing deep river valleys on dramatic bridges, and emerging at a town that sits at the crossroads of geology and mythology.

Takachiho is regarded in Japanese mythology as the place where Amano Iwato — the cave where the sun goddess Amaterasu hid, plunging the world into darkness — is located. The Amano Iwato Shrine (天岩戸神社) sits at the entrance to this sacred cave, and the nearby Amano Yasukawara cave, where the gods are said to have gathered to lure Amaterasu out, is one of the most atmospheric spiritual sites in Japan.

The gorge itself is a narrow volcanic canyon carved by the Gokase River through columnar basalt formations. The Manai Falls (真名井の滝) plunge 17 meters from the cliff face directly into the gorge, and you can rent a small rowboat to paddle beneath them. The hexagonal basalt columns that line the gorge walls are a geological wonder — perfectly geometric formations created by the slow cooling of ancient lava flows.

Continuing east from Takachiho, the road descends toward the Miyazaki coast through increasingly lush subtropical forest, eventually reaching the city of Nobeoka and the Pacific Ocean.

Campervan tips: The mountain roads between Aso and Takachiho are narrow in places (single lane with passing areas) and require careful driving in a campervan. Drive slowly and use your horn on blind corners as locals do. Takachiho has a large public parking area near the gorge entrance that accommodates campervans. The town is compact and walkable once parked.

4. Nichinan Coast (日南海岸)

Route: Miyazaki City → Nichinan → Kushima  |  Distance: ~80 km  |  Driving time: 1.5–2 hours

The Nichinan Coast along National Route 220 is Kyushu's answer to the Pacific Coast Highway — a spectacular coastal road that hugs the cliffs above the deep blue Pacific Ocean for nearly 80 kilometers. The drive south from Miyazaki City reveals a series of natural wonders that feel almost tropical, reflecting Kyushu's southern latitude and the warming influence of the Kuroshio Current.

The route's first major landmark is Aoshima Island (青島), a tiny island connected to the mainland by a short bridge, surrounded by the remarkable "Devil's Washboard" (鬼の洗濯板) — wave-cut rock platforms of Miyazaki Group sandstone and mudstone that extend in perfectly parallel ridges for hundreds of meters along the coast. The geological precision of these formations is mesmerizing, and they are best viewed at low tide.

Further south, the road reaches Sun Messe Nichinan, home to the only authorized replicas of Easter Island's Moai statues outside of Rapa Nui itself. Seven full-size Moai stand on a grassy clifftop overlooking the Pacific — a surreal and surprisingly moving sight.

The road continues through the historic town of Obi (飫肥), known as the "Little Kyoto of Kyushu" for its well-preserved samurai district and castle ruins. The local specialty, obi-ten (a type of fried fish cake), is worth stopping for.

Near the southern end of the route, Toi Misaki (都井岬) is home to a herd of wild horses — the Misaki-uma — that have roamed the cape's grasslands for over 300 years. Watching wild horses graze on a windswept Pacific cape is an unforgettable experience.

Campervan tips: National Route 220 is a well-maintained two-lane road suitable for all campervans. Parking at Aoshima, Sun Messe, and Toi Misaki is ample. The coastal scenery is best in the morning light when the sun illuminates the cliffs. Michi-no-Eki Nango and Michi-no-Eki Phoenix along this route offer convenient overnight stops with ocean views.

5. Shimabara Peninsula Loop (島原半島一周)

Route: Unzen → Shimabara → Obama → Unzen  |  Distance: ~75 km  |  Driving time: 2–2.5 hours

The Shimabara Peninsula in Nagasaki Prefecture is dominated by Mount Unzen (雲仙岳), a volcanic complex that erupted catastrophically in 1991, creating massive pyroclastic flows that tragically killed 43 people, including three volcanologists. Today, the peninsula offers a unique blend of geological education, hot spring culture, and coastal beauty.

The drive begins with the ascent to Unzen Onsen, a mountain hot spring resort at approximately 700 meters elevation. The town is surrounded by the Unzen Jigoku (Unzen Hells) — a compact area of violently boiling hot springs, hissing fumaroles, and sulfurous steam vents that you can walk through on a boardwalk path. The sulfur smell is intense, and the ground literally shakes beneath your feet.

From Unzen, the road descends to the east coast town of Shimabara, a former castle town with a beautifully reconstructed white castle and a charming network of spring-fed canals where koi fish swim through the streets. The Gamadasu Dome disaster museum here provides a sobering and educational account of the 1991 eruption, with preserved damage and multimedia presentations.

The coastal road south and west passes through fishing villages and fertile farmland before reaching Obama Onsen (小浜温泉), a seaside hot spring town famous for having the hottest spring water in Japan (105°C at the source). The town's 105-meter-long free foot bath along the waterfront — the longest in Japan — is a highlight. Soak your feet while watching the sunset over the Ariake Sea.

Campervan tips: The mountain road to Unzen has moderate switchbacks but is wide enough for campervans. Unzen town has a large public parking area near the Jigoku. The coastal roads are straightforward and scenic. Obama's waterfront parking lot is excellent for an evening stop (foot bath open until 24:00). Michi-no-Eki Mizunashi Honjin Fukkamura near Shimabara offers an excellent overnight stop with views of the volcano.

6. Sakurajima Volcanic Loop (桜島一周)

Route: Sakurajima Port → Full island loop  |  Distance: ~35 km  |  Driving time: 1–1.5 hours

Driving around Sakurajima — one of Japan's most active volcanoes — is a surreal experience that has no parallel anywhere else in the world. This volcano erupts hundreds of times per year (sometimes daily), regularly showering nearby Kagoshima City with volcanic ash. Yet approximately 4,000 people live on its slopes, farming the rich volcanic soil that produces the world's largest daikon radishes and the world's smallest mikans (mandarin oranges).

You reach Sakurajima via a 15-minute ferry ride from Kagoshima Port (the ferry runs 24 hours and accommodates campervans). From the port, a loop road circles the entire volcano, offering constantly changing perspectives of the smoking crater, lava fields from historical eruptions, and views across Kinko Bay to Kagoshima City.

Key stops along the loop include the Yunohira Observatory (湯之平展望所), the closest public viewpoint to the crater at 373 meters elevation, where you can feel the volcanic rumbling beneath your feet. The Arimura Lava Observatory (有村溶岩展望所) provides a walking trail through solidified lava from the massive 1914 eruption — the same eruption that connected Sakurajima (formerly an island) to the Osumi Peninsula. And the Kurokami Buried Shrine Gate (黒神埋没鳥居) shows a Shinto torii gate buried up to its crossbar in volcanic ash from that same eruption — a powerful visual reminder of nature's force.

At the island's northeastern tip, a free natural foot bath fed by volcanic hot springs offers a perfect rest stop with views across the bay.

Campervan tips: The Sakurajima ferry from Kagoshima Port accommodates campervans (approximately ¥1,600 for a standard campervan, one-way). The loop road is flat, wide, and easy to drive. Multiple parking areas at each viewpoint are campervan-friendly. Consider timing your loop to end at the Yunohira Observatory for sunset — the views of Kagoshima City and Kinko Bay bathed in golden light are extraordinary. Note: on heavy eruption days, volcanic ash may necessitate using your windshield wipers and keeping windows closed.

7. Kunisaki Peninsula (国東半島)

Route: Usa → Kunisaki → Beppu  |  Distance: ~100 km  |  Driving time: 2.5–3 hours

The Kunisaki Peninsula in northeastern Oita Prefecture is the hidden gem of Kyushu — a circular, mountainous landmass that juts into the Seto Inland Sea, covered in dense forest and dotted with ancient stone Buddhist temples and sculptures. While tourists flock to Beppu and Yufuin, Kunisaki remains almost completely unknown to international visitors, offering an authenticity and tranquility that is increasingly rare in Japan.

The driving route begins at Usa Jingu (宇佐神宮), the head shrine of all 40,000 Hachiman shrines in Japan and one of the most important Shinto sites in the country. The shrine complex sits in a forest of ancient camphor trees and is well worth an hour's exploration.

From Usa, the road winds into the peninsula's mountainous interior, passing through a landscape that feels almost medieval. Fukiji Temple (富貴寺) houses the oldest wooden structure in Kyushu — a small, exquisite Amida Hall built in the late Heian period (12th century) surrounded by ginkgo trees that turn brilliant gold in November. Nearby, Makiodo (真木大堂) preserves some of Kyushu's finest Buddhist sculptures in a simple thatched-roof hall.

The Kumano Magaibutsu (熊野磨崖仏) are enormous Buddhist figures carved directly into a cliff face in the 12th century — the largest stone cliff carvings in Japan. Reaching them requires a short but steep hike up natural stone steps through dense forest, an experience that feels like stepping back in time a thousand years.

The coastal road along the peninsula's eastern shore offers views across the Seto Inland Sea to Shikoku on clear days, before descending to Beppu and its hot springs.

Campervan tips: The interior mountain roads of Kunisaki are narrow and winding — drive with caution and be prepared to reverse to passing points when meeting oncoming traffic. The coastal road is wider and easier. Temple parking areas can be tight; the smaller the campervan, the easier this route will be. Michi-no-Eki Kunisaki near the coast provides a good overnight stop. This route is best done on a weekday when traffic is minimal.

Essential Driving Tips for Kyushu

  • Drive on the left. Japan drives on the left side of the road. If you are accustomed to driving on the right, take extra care at intersections and when exiting parking lots.
  • Toll roads vs. free routes. Kyushu has an expressway network with tolls. For scenic driving, however, the free national and prefectural routes are almost always more beautiful and pass through more interesting towns. Budget extra time for the free routes.
  • Fuel up regularly. In rural Kyushu, gas stations can be far apart and many close by 7:00 PM. Fill your tank whenever you drop below half.
  • Check volcanic alerts. Routes near Aso, Sakurajima, and Unzen can be closed during elevated volcanic activity. Check local authority websites before driving.
  • Mountain weather changes fast. The highland routes (Yamanami Highway, Aso Panorama Line) can go from clear skies to dense fog in minutes. Carry patience and wait for conditions to improve — the views are worth it.
  • Speed limits are low. Most Kyushu scenic roads have speed limits of 40–50 km/h. This is a feature, not a bug — it gives you time to absorb the scenery and react safely to the curves.

Kyushu's scenic roads are not merely transportation corridors connecting tourist attractions. They are the attraction. The drive itself — the play of light on volcanic slopes, the sudden reveal of a caldera vista around a blind curve, the smell of sulfur mixing with cedar forest — is the experience that transforms a vacation into something you will remember for a lifetime. And there is no better way to experience it than from behind the wheel of your own campervan, free to stop, linger, and wonder.
